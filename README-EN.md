# **Delay Has Become This Company’s Operating Model**

## — Unpaid wages, lapsed social insurance, withheld severance, and endless empty promises: XX Data’s problem is no longer just cash flow

A company can face difficulties.
It can go through periods of pressure.
It can struggle in a downturn and search for a way out.

But **difficulty is not an excuse for withholding what is owed, and pressure is not a license to abandon responsibility.**

If a company:

* **fails to pay employees for two consecutive months;**
* **stops contributing to social insurance and housing provident funds for several months;**
* **lays people off but keeps delaying legally required severance payments;**
* **still owes wages to employees who have already resigned;**
* **once persuaded employees to pay out of pocket for so-called “company shares” or “internal opportunities,” only to now find various excuses not to honor them;**
* **responds to legitimate questions about these matters not with clear solutions, but with emotional pressure such as “we must survive together with the company,” “think bigger,” and “everyone should understand during hard times”;**

then what is exposed is no longer merely a temporary business problem.

It is something more fundamental:

# **a persistent evasion of responsibility.**

---

# 1. What Should Be Honored First Is Not a Concept, but Payroll

XX Data has always been good at telling stories.

It talks about trends, transformation, intelligence, platforms, ecosystems, and the future.
One concept follows another.
In presentations, it always appears to stand at the forefront of the industry.
In meetings and public narratives, it can make “the future” sound grand and inspiring.

But what employees are actually experiencing is far more direct:

* Wages are delayed again and again;
* Social insurance and housing provident fund contributions remain unpaid for months;
* Payday moves from “this week” to “next week,” and then to “as soon as possible”;
* Clear promises keep dissolving into vague wording.

The true character of a company is not found in its promotional materials,
nor in the future painted by its executives,
but in whether it honors its most basic obligations.

**Paying wages on time is not a benefit. It is a baseline.**
**Contributing to social insurance and housing provident funds is not a favor. It is a legal duty.**

If even these basics cannot be secured,
then no matter how grand the narrative sounds,
it is ultimately hollow.

---

# 2. Projects That Remain at the Demo Stage Cannot Justify Making Employees Pay for “the Future”

Another recurring problem at XX Data lies in its projects and products.

On the surface, the company appears to have created many “new things”:

* Sleek interfaces;
* Complete-looking demos;
* Advanced-sounding solutions;
* Presentation decks that impress at first glance;
* Concepts packaged in cutting-edge language.

But whether those products can truly operate in real customer environments,
whether they form a complete business loop,
and whether they can support sustained use over time,
is a different matter altogether.

The deeper issue is that many projects seem to be:

* **better suited for demonstrations than real deployment;**
* **better for reports than actual delivery;**
* **better at creating the illusion of capability than solving concrete problems.**

In other words,
the company is better at producing **“a future that can be shown”**
than **“a present that can actually work.”**

What makes this especially ironic is that
the company fails to deliver not only on products,
but also on its obligations to employees.

**Concepts can be postponed. Demos can be rebuilt.
But wages, benefits, and living expenses do not pause along with them.**

---

# 3. Layoffs Without Compensation, Resignations Without Final Pay: The Delay Continues Even After People Leave

If unpaid wages for current employees can still be dressed up as a so-called “temporary arrangement,”
then **delayed severance for laid-off employees**
and **unpaid final wages for people who have already resigned**
reveal something much more telling about the company’s attitude toward responsibility.

While employees are still working, they are told:

> “The company is going through a difficult time. Please understand.”

After they are laid off:

> “Severance will be handled together later.”

After they resign:

> “Your wages will be arranged. You will not lose a cent.”

They all sound like promises.
But if every promise comes without a date, without a plan, and without fulfillment,
then it is not an arrangement.

It is **delay**.

What is even more disappointing is that
these people are no longer part of the company.
They will not share in the so-called “future upside.”
They are no longer participating in the company’s supposed growth.
And yet the labor they already provided,
and the compensation they are legally owed,
remain indefinitely suspended.

**People may leave, but the debt does not disappear with them.**
**The employment relationship may end, but the company’s obligations do not.**

---

# 4. Asking Employees to Pay for “Phantom Shares,” Then Refusing to Honor Them

Compared with unpaid wages and delayed severance,
the practice of asking employees to **pay out of pocket for so-called shares, equity, or internal investment opportunities**,
only to later avoid clear fulfillment,
is even harder to accept.

Because this is not merely a failure to pay.

It is an additional exploitation of trust.

Why would employees pay in?

Usually because they believe:

* the company has a future;
* leadership can be trusted;
* this is a genuine internal opportunity;
* sacrificing a little now may lead to future returns.

But when reality becomes:

* employees paid first;
* their rights and interests remain unconfirmed;
* fulfillment keeps being postponed;
* whenever they ask for concrete resolution, the company offers new excuses;
* eventually even explanations become evasive and ambiguous;

then this is no longer just “business difficulty.”
It becomes a second injury to employee trust.

Unpaid wages overdraw employees’ present lives.
Unfulfilled stock promises overdraw their belief in the future.

**Their reality is delayed.
Their future is delayed too.**

---

# 5. The Most Offensive Part Is Not the Hardship, but Using “Live and Die With the Company” to Silence Employees

When a company is genuinely struggling,
employees may choose to understand part of that reality.

But “understanding” never means:

* giving up wages;
* giving up social insurance and provident fund contributions;
* giving up severance;
* giving up money already invested;
* giving up the right to ask questions.

And it certainly does not mean
that employees who demand their legitimate rights
should be accused of:

* lacking loyalty;
* lacking a broader vision;
* refusing to “share the burden”;
* caring only about their personal interests.

That is emotional coercion.

When employees ask:

> “When will salaries actually be paid?”

the company replies:

> “You need to believe in the company.”

When employees ask:

> “When will social insurance and housing fund contributions be made up?”

the company replies:

> “This is a critical period. We all need to get through it together.”

When employees ask:

> “What about severance, unpaid final wages, and the money we were asked to put into company stock?”

the company replies:

> “Everyone needs to live and die with the company.”

That is not communication.

That is **shifting the consequences of management failure onto employees,
and then demanding gratitude from them for bearing the cost.**

People who truly choose to stand with a company
should not be used as a financial cushion.
And a company genuinely worthy of trust
would never turn “trust” into a tool for refusing accountability.

---

# 6. Delay Is No Longer an Isolated Incident. It Has Become a Management Habit

When wages are unpaid,
social insurance contributions are suspended,
severance is delayed,
resigned employees remain unpaid,
employee-funded equity is not honored,
and every response comes back to the same phrases:

* next week;
* as soon as possible;
* still being processed;
* funding is being arranged;
* please hold on a little longer;

then outsiders are not looking at a one-time mistake.

They are looking at a sustained management pattern:

# **Delay whatever can be delayed.

Blur whatever can be blurred.
Let employees carry the burden whenever possible.**

That is the truly dangerous part.

A company does not necessarily begin to collapse
the moment its financial statements deteriorate.

It begins to collapse when:

* promises become increasingly cheap;
* employees are increasingly brushed aside;
* responsibility is treated with growing indifference;
* delay becomes normalized.

---

# 7. A Company May Fail to Grow Quickly, but It Cannot Be Allowed to Lose Basic Accountability

Not every company succeeds.
Not every product reaches commercialization.
Startups can fail.
Markets can shift.
Judgment calls can be wrong.

These things can be understood.

What cannot be excused is:

* using employee wages to preserve the company’s appearance of stability;
* withholding social insurance contributions to buy time on the balance sheet;
* delaying severance to reduce layoff pressure;
* continuing to consume employee trust through “internal stock opportunities”;
* using “shared survival” rhetoric to block legitimate questions.

**A business model can fail.
Responsibility cannot disappear.**

**Strategy can change.
Existing obligations cannot be rewritten.**

---

# Conclusion: What Has Been Withheld Is Not Just Money

What XX Data has taken from employees
is not only two months of wages,
not only several months of social insurance and provident fund contributions,
and not only delayed compensation or unresolved employee investments.

It has also withheld:

* trust in the company;
* the basic expectation of professional dignity;
* the last remaining belief that a promise still means something.

The most frightening thing about a company
is not that it temporarily runs short of cash,
but that everyone gradually realizes:

> **It is not merely unable to honor its commitments for the moment.
> It has grown accustomed to avoiding responsibility.**

When “next week” becomes routine,
when “please wait a little longer” becomes policy,
when “we must survive together” becomes a way to evade accountability,
then what this company needs to examine
is no longer its fundraising, market positioning, strategy, or concepts.

It needs to ask itself:

# **Does it still deserve the trust its employees once gave it?**
